I'm Nicholas, I'm based in the UK and work as a principal engineer at Anaconda, where I'm a member of the wonderful PyScript team.

But this is going to be a very personal talk, and nothing to do with my day job.

In this talk, I'm going to use music as a metaphor, a contrast and a foil to coding. It's an opportunity to explore, re-evaluate and re-encounter coding from an unusual and hopefully stimulating perspective. I'll be paying particular attention to teaching, learning and the place of software within our culture. I hope you enjoy it and find it interesting.

But before embarking, I want to learn a bit about you.

Part 1.
The Knowledge

With the scene suitably set, let's start by exploring different types of skill, knowledge and ways to pay attention to the world. These are, after all, the sorts of things we need to learn about in order to do meaningful stuff.

We start with a musical example.

Nature

There are many elements that make up music and one of the more important is the concept of pitch. How do we know what pitches to play? We use the naturally occuring harmonic series in a circular process that give us our 12 note scale. Pythagoras is, traditionally, said to have discovered these relationships. Instruments are then tuned - their physical characteristics are changed - so they play the notes arrived at via these mathematical relationships.

In computing, an equivalent might be to describe the mechanical processes of a Turing machine as a way to define a computer.

The repeated steps of reading a symbol from a tape and looking up a machine's numbered state, to discover a next step that defines updating or ignoring the symbol at the current position on the tape, transitioning to a new machine state and moving the tape one way or another is, unambiguously, the foundations of computing.

What's important, is we are describing aspects of and processes relating to a purely physical view of the world. Yet our world is so much richer than just such a view because we can't help but give meaning to those aspects of the physical world we've just been exploring.

Meaning

What do I mean?

In music we group pitches into keys, we describe the relationship between pitches with "intervals" and such intervals are categorised as either consonant or dissonant.

In computing sequences of bits are used to represent things. We even have to agree on the "endian-ness" to make sure we get the order of bits the right way round when reading or writing memory, eating eggs, or transmitting data via a network. You don't need me to tell you the on/off state of bits is used to represent binary numbers, which themselves represent other things... such as characters in a string of text.

Introduce Mu

>>> ord("A")
65
>>> chr(65)
'A'
>>> bin(65)
'0b1000001'

Keys, consonance or dissonance, endianness or that the number 65 represents the character "A" are not true facts in a scientific sense: they are meaningful concepts that map onto the physical world in some way, but most definitely are the result of the designs, choices or traditions of humans. Philosophers would describe them as socially constructed facts rather than "laws of nature".

Abstraction

Not only do we ascribe meaning to things, but we combine and build with such relationships to create more complicated things. In other words, we make use of abstraction.

Here's a couple of musical abstractions:

If melody is pitches of a certain duration ordered through time, then harmony concerns pitches played together at the same time.

In other words, melody is the X axis, harmony is the Y.

The intervals we encountered earlier allow us to build chords (that is, notes played together as a harmony) in a certain key and the consonance / dissonance categories allow us to resolve chords into one another.

The notion of chords and harmonic resolution depends on the lower level concepts of pitch, keys and intervals.

print("Hello, world!")

Similarly, the way we ascribed numbers to characters allows us to make groups of such numbers that represent strings of textual information... for example, Python code that has a certain formal syntax that allows us to combine meaningful abstractions.

Art(efacts)

Such abstractions, when sufficiently powerful, allow us to express ourselves, create cultural artefacts that influence and interact with people.

For example, remember that hymn like example from just a moment ago. Here's the same chord sequence, but re-imagined by a dude called Johann Sebastian Bach.

  • Chords become broken chords - feeling of movement and flow.
  • Texture - how the notes are arranged together give a feeling of distance: either squashed together or spaced wide apart.
  • Rubato, articulation, dynamics, phrasing: Used to create a performer's tone of voice (like an actor).

This piece of music is brought into the world by the imagination of Bach and my wiggling my fingers so as to hit the right notes.

Python code, made through the imagination of the programmer, is also brought into the world by executing it on a computer: it interacts with the wider world in some valuable, entertaining or other significant way.

Culture

And all this happens within a cultural context that informs our decisions and reflects our unique personal tastes, backgrounds and, dare I say it, prejudices.

We stand on the shoulders of giants, and who we choose to celebrate as our giants is itself a reflection of our culture and values. Here's a slide with lots of dead German dudes - which is a pretty narrow minded view of the rich and diverse world of music. But this is synonymous with...

...a slide with lots of rich white men (who don't actually write that much code).

Ten years ago it was all the rage to "inspire" kids with the view that they should aspire to make as much money as possible as coders.

I believe this is a very narrow-minded, simplistic and not particularly helpful way to encourage folks to code. In fact it's quite mercenary and frames the value of code in terms of money...

May I venture a more enlarged version of the previous slide?

  • Ada Lovelace - the world's first computer programmer.
  • Sophie Wilson - designer of the ARM architecture.
  • Alan Turing - formalised the definition of a computer.
  • Annie Easley - one of the first computer/rocket scientists at NASA.

I hope we can all agree, a more enlightened and nuanced view promotes a deeper and more subtle view of coding as an art or rewarding skill practiced by all sorts of interesting people (not just billionaires) that allows you to make cool things, rather than how much money you can make from it.

The important aspect of culture that I want to highlight, is that it's diverse, which means context and an awareness of context matters.

This may strike you as not a particularly pleasant chord...

There are different styles of music. You like jazz, I like classical.

Each brings a different aspect of the world to light.

If you try to analyse such things, you're doing it wrong because we're talking about culture, values, politics, ethics, aesthetics, feelings and expression.

Such attention is also relevant to programming languages - each one presents a different attitude or way to pay attention to the world, that in turn affects and changes those who use such languages.

Take, for instance, the different aesthetics of Perl and Python. Any many respects they're similar languages: high level, running on virtual machines, written as collaborative open source projects with large international communities. Yet we have the Zen of Python and Perl users like to introduce people to the friend Tim. Tim Towtdi (There is more than one way to do it).

Who's right?

Trick question.... nobody's right... they're just different and perhaps we should let many flowers bloom. Diversity is a strength, right..?

Part 2.
The Journey

We've only briefly explored the many different aspects, skills, knowledge and ways to pay attention to and interact with the world.

Anyone who claims learning music is simply limited to acquiring the ability to read notes, is just as ignorant as someone claiming that learning coding merely involves learning what a "while" loop is.

Clearly, the world, and us humans who inhabit it, is a complicated place.

It turns out that, given such a rich, deep and complex situation, learning anything to any degree of competence requires a long term investment of time and effort to acquire a set of skills, theory, knowledge and experience that have been understood enough such that folks know how to effectively apply them to whatever it is that they find meaningful.

This is a journey to experience.

In a musical sense, how do we help this young violinist explore what music has to offer, encounter the support she needs to build the confidence required to grow into the musician she wants to become.

How do we get from this...

...to perhaps this. Nicola Benedetti, the Scottish concert violinist.

...or this? Just a group of friends, amateur musicians, coming together for a session in the pub.

...or this? Chineke: a world class orchestra created by and for classical musicians from under-represented ethnic minority groups.

...or this? Kala Ramnath, the grammy award winning Hindustani violinist who comes from a long line of traditional Indian classical musicians.

...or this? Jasser Haj Youssef, a Tunisian violinist, musicologist, composer and performer who crosses genres by playing western classical, Jazz, north African and Arabian music.

...or even this? This is "Apocalyptica"... how can I put this, they're a cello quartet from Finland who play metal. They're awesome.

Music represents

Each musician embodies a different way to make music. Their efforts, training, understanding, creativity and imagination enriches our world.

So how DO you get from the beginner fiddler to any one of these musicians?

I'm going to answer this question by telling you about my journey to becoming a professional orchestral tuba player...

I attended something called "Octubafest" organised by a lady called Rachel. The aim of Octubafest is to inspire folks to fall in love with a full stack instrumental framework called tubas. They organize free tuba workshops, create online tuba tutorials and curate amazing first experiences with sousaphones.

It was a blast..!

My tuba mentor at Octubafest suggested a few books to read, so I started with this one since it promised to get me up to speed really quickly.

But that book didn't really work for me, so I bought this one instead. At over 800 pages I was certain I'd be able to learn everything I needed to play the tuba.

But I have to admit, I tried reading this book, but I couldn't really connect with the material. It felt too complicated and I even started to doubt the tuba was for me.

I have to admit, that's when I started watching videos about an alternative, more light-weight musical instrument framework called "the recorder".

It soon became apparent that my calling was for the bass end of things so I soon abandoned the recorder, which was a lot more difficult to play than I at first imagined.

A friend knew someone who was an actual musician with a degree in music theory, and they suggested I look into the technical details of music.

Apparently, in actual real-life music auditions you have to solve harmony problems on a white board, so this seemed like a really good idea.

So I found a series of two minute videos about music theory.

But none of it made any sense.

Music theory makes quantum mechanics look like an episode of Peppa Pig.

It was then I spotted a poster on the London underground for a bootcamp. If I handed over several thousand of pounds, and lived off my savings for three months, the bootcamp would teach me everything I needed to know.

They even told me they had an 80% success rate at placing graduates of the course in the music industry within one year of completing.

I learned about all sorts of things I never encountered in the books or video courses:

  • How to read musical notes.
  • How to play the core seven notes all musicians use on my Tuba.
  • How to wear a tuxedo at an orchestra concert.
  • Turning the pages of the score without making them rustle.
  • Bowing during applause.
  • The tutors even flagged advanced concepts like key signatures and time signatures, but said we'd pick them up better, once we'd found our first role.

Out of the cohort of 50 who started the course, only 5 of us actually finished, and of those, 4 of us do have jobs relating to music.

I imagine many of us own SAMS 24h and O'Reilly books. Octubafest is a real thing, totally awesome and run by the effervescent Rachel Coles. Sarah Jeffery's "Team Recorder" channel and Jesse Strickland's "Two Minute Music Theory" channel are wonderful examples of two formidably talented musicians who mentor through a digital medium. I wanted to show you how all the sorts of things we do in coding education; the books, Django Girls, beginner coding videos and computer science channels appear to non-programmers.

Many conscientious, passionate and generous hearted people in our community give up their time and put a huge amount of effort to make many wonderful educational resources and events.

But something doesn't quite feel right to me...

The epitome is in the the bootcamp slide. It's of an actual London based boot camp's website with references to code replaced by references to equivaent musical terms. I have to ask, would you hire a band of musicians to play at your wedding, funeral, bar mitzvah if you knew they were recent graduates of a three month music boot camp? In a sense, we're setting up folks to encounter very uncomfortable situations, and over the past two years several investigative journalists have examined and found serious problems with the claims of such courses. A good place to start would be Business Insider's piece from last year.

I think my unease is with the speed at which we seem to think folks will learn coding, and that we provide didactic, expert led, courses, books and videos. We even use euphemisms like "zero to thirty", as if we're in some sort of a grand prix. I think this is unfair to beginner coders, naive at best, and morally suspect at worse. We're simply not being truthful about the amount of time, work, effort and, let's be honest here, frustration, it takes to even start to come to terms with coding in any meaningful way.

And we've not even touched upon the exploratory, imaginative and creative aspects of coding.

So, you're probably thinking, "ok smart-arse, what would you suggest"?

Part 3.
Embodiment

As lazy engineers (in a good sense), we can ask ourselves, "surely others have solved this problem before?" and look for examples that work.

You'll be entirely unsurprised to hear that I think we could learn a lot from how music education, at its most effective, is practiced.

On Friday, all three of my kids were playing in the Symphony Hall, Birmingham, as part of the national festival of music for youth.

What is that?

It's life changing performance and progression opportunities, regardless of background or musical style.

It's an opportunity to participate in, and be empowered by, high-quality musical activities.

It's free to every young person.

Most importantly, it is a process, set of practices and group of people who embody a vision of a certain sort of inclusive musical culture. Remember, music represents.

In 2011, John Pinner asked me to look into starting an education track at PyConUK. John was also a musical connoisseur and we used Music For Youth as one of our north stars, when putting together the very first kids day in 2012.

Asking what sort of education and learning our community supports is how we decide what sort of community we become.

For it is through education and learning that we engage with our future colleagues, friends and supporters.

Education isn't just explaining stuff. As educators, it is encumbant on us to embody the sort of behaviour, attitude, skill and knowledge we hope to inspire in our students.

This is a long, slow and at times frustrating journey. But it's a journey made possible because mentors, teachers, guides and other helpers step up and encourage learners on their own journey to experience. By taking steps on this journey learners encounter and practice what it is to be a Python coder in an inclusive, supportive and diverse community.

Between 2013-2015, each year, we welcomed 50 teachers and over 100 kids to our conference, to take their first steps.

I'm proud to say, we encountered many moments like these three young coders, autonomously debugging a Python script running something crazy in Minecraft.

2017 was difficult year for me.

It was made very clear, in no uncertain terms, that my contributions to the education track were not welcome, unless they were "on message". The education track no longer embodied a cosmopolitan community, in which everyone was made welcome to contribute and participate in a way that reflected our wonderfully diversity. Rather, we had to conform to a certain PR led view of Python in education in the UK.

When I carefully, politely and respectfully raised concerns, I was put in my place.

The following year, wonderful Ben Nuttall was put in charge of the education track.

Here's Ben allaying my fears in a way that is kind, thoughtful and, importantly, embodies just the sort of community we want to encourage through an education track.

I think you should delete that tweet. You're suggesting @PyConUK is not a welcoming place.

Yet things soon took an unfortunate turn.

I don't think the sender of this clearly unwelcoming message was doing it to make a weird ironic joke.

I want to be very clear: the UK Python community and PyCon UK is full of many wonderful people, who selflessly throw themselves into the community, who bring out the best in each other and who show mutual respect, tolerance and support despite our inevitable differences created from our wonderful diversity.

Yet, my experience of PyCon UK was that I felt unwelcome.

Furthermore, I think it unhelpful to ignore folks who have encountered problems in our community. I believe we should acknowledge that not everyone has a wonderful time in the Python community. If we delete such things, we ignore problems, and that can't lead anywhere good. It also means we're not embodying what it is to be a welcoming, inclusive, tolerant and supportive community.

The Python community has many wonderful aspects to it.

The Python community also contains folks who exhibit behaviour that make it hard to be a member of this community.

This was my in-box a few months ago. I wasn't the only Mu maintainer to be on the receiving end of such email.

Clearly this behaviour is unacceptable and all of us involved in Mu hope the sender is in a better place so they're able to understand the affects of such emails.

By 2018 I had run out of steam.

The ill judged, thoughtless and sometimes nasty interactions I encountered in the Python community felt like death by a 1000 paper cuts.

I was sitting on top of years of the very worst the Python community had to offer.

One day, I reposted an announcement from the PSF about something or other... I think it was related to fund raising.

It contained a spelling mistake or some grammatical errors. In any case, the volunteer who wrote it clearly hadn't proof read it. Busy people are busy and sometimes they don't catch every little mistake. I certainly missed it.

But I was contacted by a member of the UK Python community who decided to complain in a way that was both unkind and rude about the original author of the announcement.

That was the straw that broke the camel's back.

Sitting on a decade of pent up frustration I tweeted.

Many folks in the UK Python community replied with compassion, thoughfulness and support. They are the best of us.

I am a senior member of the UK Python community. How dare you bad mouth our community on an international platform.

This response, since deleted, reduced me to tears.

I was, once again, being put in my place. Clearly I had missed the PSF board resolution that said the Python community was now hierarchical with senior and other levels of status.

I replied that I was sorry this person felt so angry, but that I was dealing with a lot of pent up sadness and frustration. Clearly, the UK Python community had taken a toll on my mental health.

A far flung friend pointed out that my comments should be taken as a gift of constructive criticism. I was just about to respond with thanks, when another UK based Python organiser replied to say that my comments were in fact passive aggressive and my intent was clearly to harm the community. Then other decided to join in along similar lines.

It doesn't matter who said these things. I'm taking great care to avoid pointing fingers or apportioning blame. That really would be unhelpful. Rather I want to highlight behaviour in the Python community that makes it difficult for folks on the receiving end of things, to remain in the community.

Crushed

Humiliated

Despondent

How did this experience make me feel?

Crushed, because a community that prides itself on being welcoming, inclusive and supportive, clearly wasn't.

Much of this played out on social media. I'm actually a very shy and private person, despite who you think you see on stage at this moment in time. I no longer use social media as a direct result of my interactions with folks in the UK Python community.

If my sadness due to the death by a thousand paper cuts wasn't enough, this series of events was a tipping point. Since 2018, I have had no contact with the "formal" UK Python community or PyCon UK, although, of course, I keep in touch with friends.

I still carry a huge amount of sadness and pain about how things turned out. For what it's worth, I hope people in the UK Python community never treat one of their own like this ever again.

Hope?

Sometimes when folks encounter such aspects of the Python community they repond by saying we should be positive about Python, we should emphasise and celebrate the good stuff.

You should be hopeful, Nicholas.

Here's what I hope:

When we're together in this community, especially if things go wrong, we acknowledge and work through differences. In so doing, we transform and enlarge each other.

To nurture such mutual growth, I hope our community is a place of learning, understanding and sharing. I hope our community is a place to affirm tolerance and compassion. I hope our community is a place for collaboratove creativity.

All the diverse aspects of ourselves we choose to bring to this community should be welcome. Reciprocate! Welcome, embrace and learn from others (even in the face of apparent misunderstanding).

To act against this spirit invites ostracism... a loss that diminishes us all.

Part 4.
Expression

If our community is not a safe place in which to express yourself then we extinguish any possibility of you becoming fully a part of the community. In so doing we are all diminished: your contribution is curtailed, your unique gifts are cast aside and you have no way to become fully yourself as a member of the community.

Why is this so important?

Code isn't written for computers.

We write code because we are human beings, imbued with ideas, imagination and feelings. Code expresses our thoughts made digital: a medium for our values, cultures and ways of life.

In other words, we are expressing ourselves when we write code.

The word "express" means to manifest or make known. When we write code we express our vision of what a digital world should look like or behave. It reflects who we are. It reflects where we come from. It reflects how we want to be in the world.

A last musical metaphor...

In a recent interview with Chinese American cellist explains,

"The job of a musician is to transcend technique in order to express something of value. We explore lots of different music through our instruments and we love it because it allows us to encounter different states of mind."

As developers, as we write code, we're trying to express something of value. We're exploring and interacting with our world through code and the applications we write.

But what does he mean by transcend technique?

At the start we examined lots of different aspects of knowledge and paying attention to the world. The sorts of things we learn about when we become musicians or coders.

We talked about the physics that tunes this instrument, the organisation of notes, some of which are consonant or dissonant, how the context of chords gives us harmony, how arpeggio, tempo, dynamics and rubato are used to make a piece sound interesting. These are techniques, just like those I also mentioned relating to coding.

When we express ourselves, we take all this stuff, assemble it, refine it, fix it, break it and play around with it... but in the end, and if we're in a safe place, we send it into the world so others can make sense of it.

All the things that are fundamental, all the things that, to the human spirit, are most profoundly significant, can only be experienced, not expressed. ~ Aldous Huxley
