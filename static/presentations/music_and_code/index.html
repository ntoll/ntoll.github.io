I'm Nicholas. I'm a principal engineer at Anaconda, working in the PyScript team.

This is going to be a personal talk, and nothing to do with my day job.

I'm going to use music as a metaphor, a contrast and a foil to coding. It's an opportunity to explore, re-evaluate and re-encounter coding from an unusua l and hopefully stimulating perspective. I'll be paying particular attention to teaching, learning and the place of software within our culture. I hope you enjoy it.

But first, I want to learn about you.

You..?

Any musicians in the audience? I must apologise in advance for my piano playing.

I'm really an orchestral tuba player and piano is very much my second study. For non-musicians, what I've just said is something along the lines of, as Python is to my tuba playing, so copy and pasting JavaScript from StackOverflow is to my piano playing. Thus demonstrating the power of analogy and metaphor to reveal interesting insights about the world. I'll be doing a lot of that during this talk.

Any students or teachers here? I hoped you'd all put up your hands.

A wonderful yet challenging aspect of our work as engineers is that we're always learning new technology. We're lifelong students. Inevitably, if you write code, you'll also write documentation, explain code for review or to colleagues, give talks at conferences like this one or support collaborators or users of your application. A core part of being an engineer is to explain yourself, and that means you're a teacher. Pedagogy, the skill of mentoring and supporting learning, is an essential aspect of what it is to be an effective engineer.

Part 1.
Knowledge

With the scene suitably set, let's start by exploring different types of skill, knowledge and ways in which we pay attention to the world. These are, after all, the sorts of things we need to cultivate and understand in order to do meaningful things together.

We start with a musical example.

Nature

There are many elements that make up music and one of the more important is the concept of pitch. How do we know what pitches to play? We use the naturally occuring harmonic series in a circular process that give us our 12 note scale. Pythagoras is, traditionally, said to have discovered these relationships. Instruments are then tuned - their physical characteristics are changed - so they play the notes arrived at via these mathematical relationships.

In computing, an equivalent might be to describe the mechanical processes of a Turing machine as a way to define a computer.

The repetition of reading symbols from a tape and looking up a state, only to modify the tape, move it and transition to a new state, is, unambiguously, the mechanical foundation of what it is to be a computer.

What's interesting is I'm describing aspects of and processes relating to a purely physical view of the world.

Yet our world is a much richer place, because we can't help but ascribe meaning to those aspects of the physical world we've just been exploring.

Meaning

What do I mean?

In music we group pitches into keys, we describe the relationship between pitches with "intervals" and such intervals are categorised as either consonant or dissonant.

SINGING

In computing sequences of bits are used to represent things. We even have to agree on the "endian-ness" to make sure we get the order of bits the right way round when reading or writing memory, eating eggs, or transmitting data via a network. You don't need me to tell you the on/off state of bits is used to represent binary numbers, which themselves represent other things... such as characters in a string of text.

Introduce Mu

>>> ord("A")
65
>>> chr(65)
'A'
>>> bin(65)
'0b1000001'

Keys, consonance or dissonance, endianness or that the number 65 represents the character "A" are not true facts in a scientific sense: they are meaningful concepts that map onto the physical world in some way, but most definitely are the result of the designs, choices or traditions of humans. Philosophers would describe them as socially constructed facts rather than "laws of nature".

Traffic lights?

Abstraction

Not only do we ascribe meaning to things, but we combine and build with such relationships to create more complicated things. In other words, we make use of abstraction.

Here's a couple of musical abstractions:

If melody is pitches of a certain duration ordered through time, then harmony concerns pitches played together at the same time.

In other words, melody is the X axis, harmony is the Y.

The intervals we encountered earlier allow us to build chords (that is, notes played together as a harmony) in a certain key and the consonance / dissonance categories allow us to resolve chords into one another.

The notion of chords and harmonic resolution depends on the lower level concepts of pitch, keys and intervals.

There's a sort of knowledge dependency tree emerging...

print("Hello, world!")

Similarly, the way we map numbers to characters allows us to group such numbers together to build strings of textual information... for example, Python code that has a certain formal syntax that allows us to combine meaningful abstractions.

Just as with music, the notion of strings, and code expressed using a certain syntax depends upon the lower level concepts of binary numbers representing characters, with binary numbers mapped to physical characteristics of the hardware.

Art(efacts)

Such abstractions, when sufficiently powerful, allow us to express ourselves, create cultural artefacts that influence and interact with people.

For example, remember that hymn like example from just a moment ago? Here's the same chord sequence, but re-imagined by a dude called Johann Sebastian Bach.

  • Chords become broken chords - feeling of movement and flow.
  • Texture - how the notes are arranged together give a feeling of distance: either squashed together or spaced wide apart.
  • Rubato, articulation, dynamics, phrasing: Used to create a performer's tone of voice (like an actor).

This piece of music is brought into the world by the imagination of Bach and my wiggling my fingers so as to hit the right notes.

I can also wiggle my fingers to create Python code:

from microbit import *
import random
import music

greeting = random.choice(["Hello", "Bonjour", "Ciao",])
display.scroll(greeting + " EuroPython!", delay=70, wait=False)
music.play(music.POWER_UP)

This code, when executed by a computer, interacts with the wider world in some valuable, entertaining or other significant way.

Culture

And all this happens within a cultural context that informs our decisions and reflects our unique personal tastes, backgrounds and, dare I say it, prejudices.

We stand on the shoulders of giants, and who we choose to celebrate as our giants is itself a reflection of our culture and values. Here's a slide with lots of dead German dudes - which is a pretty narrow minded view of the rich and diverse world of music. But this is synonymous with...

...a slide with lots of rich white men who run tech companies.

Ten years ago it was all the rage to "inspire" kids with all the money that could be made writing code.

Personally, I believe this is not a particularly helpful way to encourage folks to learn to code. In fact it's quite mercenary and frames what we do only in terms of financial or economic worth...

To paraphrase Oscar Wilde: we're aware of the price of code, but not of its intrinsic value.

May I venture a more enlarged version of the previous slide?

  • Ada Lovelace - the world's first computer programmer.
  • Sophie Wilson - designer of the ARM architecture.
  • Alan Turing - formalised the definition of a computer.
  • Annie Easley - one of the first computer/rocket scientists at NASA.

I hope we can all agree, a more nuanced perspective promotes code as a creative act and rewarding skill practiced by all sorts of interesting people (not just billionaires). Code allows you to make cool things that reflect your world and tell your story, rather than just make shed loads of money (although that is also possible).

Diversity means context and a respect for context matters.

What do I mean..?

This may strike you as not a particularly pleasant chord...

There are different styles of music that simply reflect different cultures. You like jazz, I like classical.

Each brings a different aspect of the world to light.

If you try to analyse such things, or reduce them to neatly defined components, you're doing it wrong. These things can only be understood in the large. We're talking about the phenomenology of culture, politics, ethics, aesthetics, feelings and expression.

In other words, what does it feel like to..?

This is also relevant to programming languages - each one presents a different attitude or way to pay attention to the world, that in turn affects and changes those who use such languages.

Take, for instance, the different aesthetics of Perl and Python. In many respects they're similar languages: high level, running on virtual machines, written as collaborative open source projects with large international communities. Yet we have the Zen of Python with it's "one obvious way to do things", and Perl users like to tell people "there is more than one way to do it".

Who's right?

Trick question.... nobody's right... they're just different and perhaps we should let many flowers bloom. Diversity is a strength, right..?

Part 2.
The Journey

We've only briefly explored the many different aspects, skills, knowledge and ways to pay attention to and interact with the world.

Anyone who claims learning music is simply limited to acquiring the ability to read notes, is just as ignorant as someone claiming that learning to code merely involves understanding a programming language's syntax and core constructs.

Clearly, the world is a complicated place. As are us humans who inhabit it.

It turns out that, given such a rich, deep and complex situation, learning anything to any degree of competence requires a long term investment of time and effort to acquire a set of skills, theory, knowledge and experience. These have to be understood enough such that folks know how to effectively apply them to whatever end it is that they find meaningful.

This is a journey to experience.

In a musical sense, how do we get from this beginner violinist...

...to this. Nicola Benedetti, the Scottish concert violinist.

...or this? Aly Bain, a Shetlander and renowned folk fiddler.

...or this? Just a group of friends, amateur musicians, coming together for a session in the pub.

...or this? Chineke: a world class orchestra created by and for classical musicians from under-represented ethnic minority groups.

...or this? Kala Ramnath, the grammy award winning Hindustani violinist who comes from a long line of traditional Indian classical musicians.

...or this? Jasser Haj Youssef, a Tunisian violinist, musicologist, composer and performer who crosses genres by playing western classical, Jazz, north African and Arabian music.

...or even this? This is "Apocalyptica"... how can I put this, they're a cello quartet from Finland who play metal. They're awesome.

Music represents

Each musician embodies a different way to make music. Their efforts, training, understanding, creativity and imagination enriches our world.

So how DO you help the beginner fiddler grow into someone like any one of these musicians?

I'm going to answer this question by telling you about my recent journey to becoming a professional orchestral tuba player...

I attended something called "Octubafest" organised by a lady called Rachel. The aim of Octubafest is to inspire folks to fall in love with a full stack instrumental framework called tubas.

Octubafest organize free tuba workshops, create online tuba tutorials and curate amazing first experiences with sousaphones.

It was a blast..!

My tuba mentor at Octubafest suggested a few books to read, so I started with this one since it promised to get me up to speed really quickly.

But that book didn't really work for me...

...so I bought this one instead. At over 900 pages I was certain I'd be able to learn everything I needed to play the tuba.

But I couldn't connect with the rather advanced and complicated technical material. I even started to doubt the tuba was for me.

I have to admit, that's when I started watching videos about an alternative, more light-weight musical instrument framework called "the recorder".

It soon became apparent that my calling was for the bass end of things and I soon abandoned the recorder. It turns out that, despite being so small and lightweight, the recorder is a lot more difficult to play than I at first imagined.

A friend knew someone who was an actual musician with a degree in music theory, and they suggested I look into the technical details of music.

Apparently, in actual real-life music auditions you have to solve harmony problems on a white board, and investigating music theory seemed like a really good idea.

So I found a series of bite-sized two minute videos about music theory.

Do you have any idea what he's on about?

Music theory makes quantum mechanics look like an episode of Peppa Pig.

It was then I spotted a poster on the London underground for a bootcamp. If I handed over several thousand pounds, and lived off my savings for three months, the bootcamp would teach me everything I needed to know.

They even told me they had an 80% success rate at placing graduates of the course in the music industry within one year of completing.

I learned about all sorts of things I never encountered in the books or video courses:

  • How to read musical notes.
  • How to use my tuba to play the core seven notes all classical musicians use.
  • How to wear a tuxedo for an orchestra concert.
  • Turning the pages of the score without making them rustle in the quiet bits.
  • Bowing during applause.
  • The tutors even flagged advanced concepts like key signatures and time signatures, but said we'd pick them up better, once we'd found our first role.

Out of the cohort of 50 who started the course, only 5 of us actually graduated, and of those, 4 of us (80%) do have jobs relating to music. I work in a record shop, another is a music librarian, and two others got gigs as studio technicians, although they tell me they're not allowed to touch any of the instruments.

Joking aside...

I imagine many of us own SAMS 24h and O'Reilly books. Octubafest is a real thing, totally awesome and run by the wonderful Rachel Coles. Sarah Jeffery's "Team Recorder" channel and Jesse Strickland's "Two Minute Music Theory" channel are wonderful examples of two formidably talented musicians who mentor through a digital medium.

I wanted to show you how all the sorts of things we do in coding education; the books, Django Girls, beginner coding videos and computer science channels appear to non-programmers.

Many conscientious, passionate and generous hearted people in our community give up their time and put a huge amount of effort into making many wonderful educational resources and events.

But something doesn't quite feel right to me...

The epitome is in the the bootcamp slide. It's of an actual London based boot camp's website with references to code replaced by references to equivalent musical terms. I have to ask, would you hire a band of musicians to play at your wedding, funeral, bar mitzvah if you knew they were recent graduates of a three month music boot camp? In a sense, such schemes are playing a dangerous game: setting up folks to encounter very uncomfortable situations as they quickly swim out of their technical depth. Furthermore, over the past two years several investigative journalists have examined and found serious problems with the claims of some of the more popular bootcamps. A good place to start would be Business Insider's piece from last year.

I think my unease is with the speed at which we seem to think folks will learn coding, and that we provide didactic, expert led, courses, books and videos. We even use euphemisms like "zero to thirty", as if we're in some sort of a grand prix. I think this is unfair to beginner coders, naive at best, and morally suspect at worse. We're simply not being truthful about the amount of time, work, effort and, let's be honest here, frustration, it takes to even start to come to terms with coding in any meaningful way.

And we've not even touched upon the exploratory, imaginative and creative aspects of coding.

So, you're probably thinking, "ok smart-arse, what would you suggest"?

Part 3.
Embodiment

As lazy engineers (in a good sense), we can ask ourselves, "surely others have solved this problem before?" and look for examples that work.

You'll be entirely unsurprised to hear that I think we could learn a lot from how music education, at its most effective, is practiced.

On Friday, all three of my kids were playing in the Symphony Hall, Birmingham, as part of the national festival of music for youth.

What is that?

It's life changing performance and progression opportunities, regardless of the participants' background or musical style.

It's an opportunity to participate in, and be empowered by, high-quality musical activities.

It's free to every young person.

Most importantly, it is a process, set of practices and group of passionate people who embody a vision of a certain sort of inclusive musical culture. Remember, music represents.

In 2011, John Pinner asked me to start an education track at PyConUK. John was a musical connoisseur and we both used Music For Youth as one of our north stars, when putting together the very first kids day in 2012. John's vision was to make a place for a process, set of practices and group of people who embody a certain sort of inclusive coding culture.

Asking what sort of education and learning our community supports is how we decide what sort of community we become.

For it is through education and learning that we engage with our future colleagues, friends and supporters.

Education isn't just explaining stuff. As educators, it is encumbant on us to embody the sort of behaviour, attitude, skill and knowledge we hope to...

...inspire in our students, as takes place in Music for Youth.

This is a long, slow...

...and at times frustrating journey.

But it's a journey made possible because mentors, teachers, guides and other helpers step up and encourage learners on their own journey to experience.

By taking these steps learners encounter and practice what it is to be a Python coder...

...in an inclusive, supportive and diverse community.

Between 2012-2015, each year, we welcomed 50 teachers and over 100 kids to PyConUK.

2017 was difficult year for me.

It was made very clear, in no uncertain terms, that my contributions to the education track were not welcome, unless they were "on message".

The education track no longer embodied a cosmopolitan community, in which everyone was made welcome to contribute and participate in a way that reflected our wonderful diversity. Rather, we all had to conform to a certain corporate led view of Python in education in the UK.

When I carefully, politely and respectfully raised concerns, I was put in my place.

The following year, wonderful Ben Nuttall was put in charge of the education track. I hoped we could return to John's vision and I reached out.

Here's Ben allaying my fears in a way that is kind, thoughtful and, importantly, embodies just the sort of community we want to encourage through an education track.

I think you should delete that tweet. You're suggesting @PyConUK is not a welcoming place.

Yet things soon took an unfortunate turn.

I don't think the sender of this (clearly unwelcoming) message was doing it to make a weird paradoxical ironic joke.

I want to be very clear: the UK Python community and PyCon UK is full of many wonderful people, who selflessly throw themselves into the community, who bring out the best in each other and who show mutual respect, tolerance and support despite our inevitable differences created from our wonderful diversity.

Yet, my experience of PyCon UK was that I was made unwelcome. And interventions such as this one, are simply ill judged evidence of such a situation.

The Python community has many wonderful aspects to it.

The Python community also contains folks who exhibit behaviour that make it hard to be a member of this community. By way of example, this was my in-box a few months ago.

Back in 2018 I was sitting on top of years of the very worst that the Python community had to offer and a straw broke the camel's back.

One day, I reposted an announcement from the PSF about something or other... I think it was related to fund raising. It contained a spelling mistake and some grammatical errors. In any case, the volunteer who wrote it clearly hadn't proof read it. I had certainly missed the mistakes.

I was emailed by a member of the UK Python community who decided to complain in a way that was both unkind and rude about the original author of the announcement.

Sitting on years of pent up frustration, I needed to "come clean" and be honest about what was happening. So I tweeted.

Many folks in the UK Python community replied with compassion, thoughtfulness, acknowledgement and understanding. They are the best of us.

I am a senior member of the UK Python community. How dare you bad mouth our community on an international platform.

But this response, since deleted, deeply hurt me. I was, once again, being put in my place.

I replied that I was sorry this person felt so angry, but that I was dealing with a lot of pent up sadness and frustration and that the UK Python community had taken a toll on my mental health.

A far flung friend pointed out that my comments should be taken as a gift: constructive criticism. I was just about to respond with thanks, when another UK based Python organiser replied to say that my comments were, in fact, passive aggressive and my intent was clearly to harm. Others in the UK decided to join in along similar lines.

It doesn't matter who said those things. I'm taking great care to avoid pointing fingers or apportioning blame. That really would be unhelpful. Perhaps these folks were having a bad day, like me. Rather I want to highlight behaviour in the Python community that makes it difficult for folks on the receiving end of things, to remain in the community.

Crushed

Humiliated

Despondent

How did this experience make me feel?

Crushed, because a community that prides itself on being welcoming, inclusive and supportive, clearly wasn't, and as demonstrated by my inbox, still isn't.

Much of this played out on social media. I'm actually a very shy and private person, despite who you think you see on stage at this moment in time. I found it deeply wounding to be the target of such unkind commentary.

This series of events was a tipping point. Since 2018, I have had no contact with the "formal" UK Python community or PyCon UK.

I still carry a huge amount of sadness and pain about how things turned out. For what it's worth, I hope people in the UK Python community never treat one of their own like this ever again.

Hope?

Sometimes when folks hear stories like this one, they respond by saying we should be positive about Python, we should emphasise and celebrate the good stuff.

We should be hopeful not despondent.

This is another lesson I think the Python community could learn: if someone speaks from a place of distress or sadness, you should be respectful, not silence them or white-wash the world in a "Lego Movie" like splurge of "everything is awesome". That's just great communication, not great community.

Acknowledge & Transform

Here's what I hope:

When we're together in this community, especially if things go wrong, we acknowledge and work through differences. In so doing, we transform and enlarge each other.

To nurture such mutual growth, I hope our community is a place of learning, understanding and sharing. I hope our community is a place to affirm tolerance and compassion. I hope our community is a place for collaboratove creativity.

All the diverse aspects of ourselves we choose to bring to this community should be welcomed. Reciprocate! Welcome, embrace and learn from others (even in the face of apparent misunderstanding).

To act against this spirit invites ostracism... a loss that diminishes us all.

Part 4.
Expression

If our community is not a safe space it extinguishes any possibility of folks becoming fully a part of the community. In so doing we are all diminished: contributions are curtailed, individual's unique gifts are cast aside and there is no way to fully become yourself as a member of the community.

Why is this so important?

Code isn't written for computers, in the same way music isn't written for instruments.

We write code because we are human beings, imbued with ideas, imagination and feelings. Code expresses our thoughts made digital: a medium for our values, cultures and ways of life.

In other words, we are expressing ourselves when we write code.

Our code reflects who we are. It reflects where we come from. It reflects how we want to be in the world.

A last musical metaphor...

In a recent interview with Chinese American cellist Yo Yo Ma, he explains,

"The job of a musician is to transcend technique in order to express something of value. We explore lots of different music through our instruments and we love it because it allows us to encounter different states of mind."

As developers, as we write code, we're trying to express something of value. We're exploring and interacting with our world through code and the applications we write.

But what does he mean by transcending technique?

All the things that are fundamental, all the things that, to the human spirit, are most profoundly significant, can only be experienced, not expressed.

~ Aldous Huxley

At the start we examined lots of different aspects of knowledge and ways to pay attention to the world. The sorts of things we learn about when we become musicians or coders.

We talked about the physics that tunes this instrument, the organisation of notes, some of which are consonant or dissonant, how the context of chords gives us harmony, how arpeggio, tempo, dynamics and rubato are used to make a piece sound interesting, that our cultural context influences how we make sense of these cues.

These are what YoYo Ma means by technique, just like those aspects of computing we also explored.

When we express ourselves, we take all this stuff, assemble it, refine it, fix it, break it and play around with it... but in the end, and only if we're in a safe place, we send it into the world so others can experience it. I get to encounter a little bit of you in this thing you have created, and my world is made larger as a result.

We'll end with a listening exercise to illustrate what I mean.

The moon is shining, and it's very peaceful.

You're with a friend or someone you love.
