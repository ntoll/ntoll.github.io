Also, such problems are solved problems which often don't speak to beginner programmers.

Beginner programmers are often given simple tools.

Scratch is a wonderful example of a tool to engage beginner coders. Yet the extent to which you can get Scratch to do useful things is (necessarily) limited.

This was definitely my feeling when it came to my own similar experiences with Logo in the 1980s. While I enjoyed making the "turtle" move and I learned about concepts like functions, iterations and variable assignment, I couldn't actually do anything useful. I (like many) wanted to do real stuff (I actually used a piece of software called Communitel - running on a BBC micro - to set up a school BBS.

Alan Kay nails the situation down quite well.

Reminds me of a situation in music education.

Learning music feels like learning to code (to me).

What exactly does music education do..?

Such keyboards are easy to get started with. They make you sound "good" with little effort. They're a great "gateway" into music and ensure *everyone* gets to have a go.

An opportunity to introduce the basics / core skills.

But they're a musical dead end.

However, some kids show more interest / aptitude in music and want to take things further or get to do "the real thing". Hence we provide instrumental lessons and things like school band.

These kids are playing proper instruments, in a real band and playing real (if simplified) music. They also have a real conductor, have to "really" listen to each other in order to play together and put on real concerts at which parents pay real money for tickets.
