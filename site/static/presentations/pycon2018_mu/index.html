The problem

For whom is Mu?

Have you ever tried to teach 10 year olds to program?

Beginners have enough challenges to overcome without the tools working against them. So what's the problem here?

Tools are for developers, not for learners

Minimalism FTW

Vi/Vim's UI is "minimalist" and the learning curve is steep.

This is basically what a beginner sees when they see Vim.

I must have all the features!

Others go for a "maximalist" approach. As a beginner, do you know where to type the code or even make it run?

This is simply information as a form of intimidation.

Why not use IDLE?

Carrie Anne is director of education at the Raspberry Pi Foundation -- perhaps the most successful computing in education project in history.

Upset at the current state of affairs, Carrie Anne gave a keynote and listed "must have" features for a beginner's code editor, based on RPi's own experiences and the feedback of teachers.

What has this got to do with Mu?

A Sunday afternoon hack for micro:bit

(Based on Carrie Anne's feature list.)

I was involved in BBC micro:bit. I made the browser based Python editor. When testing people said... ergo Mu was born as a "how hard can this be?" experiment.

People like Mu!

"Please can you make it into a generic Python editor?"

Lots of people like Mu but want to use it for more than just micro;bit. Raspberry Pi stepped up and supported me in work to turn Mu into a generic Python editor.

As a result, I considered what I was trying to do with Mu. This Alan Kay quote encapsulates much of what I was aiming for.

Mu aims to be the real thing

(for beginner programmers).

Mu takes a more realistic and pragmatic approach and doesn't try to hide the effort needed (but attempts to help)..

What if we taught programming like we teach music?

In the end I turned the question I asked myself around. I have a LOT of mileage from thinking about my practice as a music educator and wondering how to apply the 500 years worth of musical pedagogical technique into something useful for learning to code.

One way to look at the mapping between the worlds of musical and coding education. Scratch/Blocks et al let you create something quickly and easily and it looks "cool", but Mu is where the "real" training is happening.

As Alan Kay said, "you have to work your ass off to figure out what the real thing is in the learner's context".

Modus Operandi:

How do you go about working out what the "real thing" is for a project like Mu?

Ask teachers what would help

I've interviewed about 20 teachers and code-club mentors about the various problems they encounter when teaching coding, as well as the most common mistakes beginners make (which could be avoided with some help).

Observe students writing Python

I've been doing kids' days at PyCon UK since 2012. I also visited about 5 code clubs in the UK and took part in Python lessons with kids in some secondary schools (I was the "captive real-life coder").

Draw upon developer experience

As developers we know the sorts of things kids could / should be learning (and often teachers base their ideas of what we do as a job on stereotypes and myths).

Experiment, test, fix (repeat)

Agile FTW.

The Essence of Mu:

  • Less is more (remove all unnecessary distractions);
  • Keep it simple (so Mu is easy to understand);
  • Walk the path of least resistance (Mu should be easy);
  • Have fun (learning should be a positive experience).

In the end, the what I learned boils down to these four points.
