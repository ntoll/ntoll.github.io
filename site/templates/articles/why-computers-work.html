Why Computers Work

Sunday, 17th December 2017 (9:30PM)

There are many explanations of computers on the internet. For example, my buddy Carrie Anne Philbin has a series of videos introducing how various aspects of computing work. Go watch them, they're wonderful videos! Here's a flavour of what you can expect.

I'm taking a very different approach in this blog post. Rather than describing "how", I'll explain "why" computers work.

What's the difference?

Answers to "how?" questions tell us what method or steps make such-and-such a thing work or happen. This is the approach taken in most explanations of computers and so successfully demonstrated by Carrie Anne in her wonderful video series.

In contrast, the answers to "why?" questions explain what makes something possible, thus providing a broader context and the potential to explore something at a more fundamental level.

It's the difference between a car mechanic who may understand how an engine works (so they can fix broken engines), and a mechanical engineer who understands the physics and chemistry of why engines work (so they can improve the design of engines). Of course, this is a simplistic metaphor and, in reality, experts understand both "how" and "why" aspects of their fields.

Nevertheless, just understanding how something works means your frame of reference is limited to within the system, whereas if you know why something works you're not limited to specific products, solutions or practices. Knowing why something is possible is an invitation to be creative, explore a problem area and exceed current solutions.

My aim is to address the inbalance of the large number of "how" explanations with a very simple exploration of "why" computers work.

Happily, the basics of why computing works are easy to understand. I'm going to give you enough information so you can delve into details should you wish to explore further.

Most importantly, be open minded. Explore, experiment and play. Let loose your imagination and don't limit yourself to thinking in terms of how things currently are.

Our exploration starts with a card game...

Following Rules

Do you know how to play Snap?

It's the sort of game very young children learn to play. It uses a standard deck of cards and the aim is to win all the cards. Here's how it works:

Players sit in a circle with one of them acting as dealer. The dealer shuffles the cards so they're in a random order. The cards are dealt, face down, to each player until the dealer has none left. Before the game begins, each player should have a neat stack of face-down cards.

The player to the left of the dealer takes the first turn.

Players take a turn by moving the top card from their face-down stack onto the top of an adjacent stack of face-up cards so the moved card is also facing up. If the face-up stack doesn't already exist a new stack is created. The player to the left always goes next.

If a player notices two face-up cards on different stacks have the same value (for example, two aces are visible) then they shout "SNAP!". The first player who correctly shouts out wins all the cards in the matching stacks of cards. The matching stacks are added to the bottom of the winner's face-down pile. Play continues to the left of the player who turned up the matching card.

If a player mistakenly shouts "SNAP!" at the wrong time they must pay a forfeit of one card from their face-down stack to each of the other players. These are added to the bottom of the other players' face-down stacks.

If two or more players shout "SNAP!" at the same time both of the matching stacks are placed in the middle and added to the "Snap Pool". When "SNAP!" is next called correctly, the winning player wins both the matching stacks and the Snap Pool.

If a player runs out of face-down cards they flip over their face-up stack and play continues as before. If a player completely runs out of cards, they are out of the game.

Play continues until the winning player holds all the cards.

These rules describe how, given a certain state of affairs, such and such things must happen. States of affairs describe how things are in the world. For example, "there are two piles of face-up cards that have cards of matching values on the top of each". When I say that such and such must happen, I mean clear and unambiguous instructions tell you how play proceeds given a certain state of affairs.

Playing the game of "Snap" means you modify your behaviour to follow the rules of the game. If everyone follows the rules then we can all play together.

We're used to following rules. Obviously, we don't explain card games in such a formal manner to very young children. It's a fun game: they learn the entertainment of skillfully following the rules to bring about exciting situations.

The computing related rules and states of affairs I'm about to describe are not much more complicated than the game of Snap explained above. However what follows may include a few "brain twists" -- where I expect you to use your imagination to see things in a seemingly unusual or unintuitive way! It's fun and can result in rather pleasant "aha" moments (that feeling you get when, if you were a cartoon character, a lightbulb would appear above your head).

I want you to remember the way it feels to follow the rules of a game like Snap when when you read what follows. Fundamentally, they're no different: given a certain state of affairs you should do such and such a thing.

What follows is the story of how we turn our thoughts into programs that instruct computers what to do.

Movement of Thought

It's possible to apply rules to thinking about things.

This is the study of Logic and applying these rules of thinking is called reasoning. Reasoning with such rules makes you rational -- others can follow the movements of thought that brought you to a certain conclusion. Furthermore, because there are rules of thought it's possible to notice when an argument doesn't make sense.

There are many types of logic but the one we're going to examine is called propositional (or sentential) logic. If this sounds complicated or a little bit intimidating, remember that one of the fun things about working with computers is that you get to speak impressive sounding technobabble. "Propositional" (or "sentential") logic is just a name that describes what it does. This type of logic concerns how sentences are formed by combining smaller propositions called premises.

Here's a simple example of propositional logic:

If it is sunny and I'm wearing a coat then I will get hot.

I want to draw your attention to some important aspects of this sentence that may not, at first, appear obvious:

  • It contains two statements called premises ("It is sunny" and "I'm wearing a coat"). Premises make assertions about states of affairs that will either be true or false.
  • It ends with a conclusion ("I will get hot"). A conclusion asserts an expected state of affairs that'll also be true or false.
  • The sentence is in the form of a conditional ("if [premises] then [conclusion]"). A conditional means that if the premises are true then the conclusion must also be true.
  • The premises are related to each other by a logical operator called conjunction ("and"). If the related premises are evaluated together, collectively they are true only if all of them are true.

Propositional logic reveals the structure of a sentence so we can understand how the argument works. Playing by the rules of logic forces everyone to reach the same inevitable conclusion: if we accept that the premises are true (it is sunny and that I'm wearing a coat) propositional logic dictates the conclusion must be true (that I will get hot).

Here's the brain twist: propositional logic doesn't care about the meaning of the overall sentence nor its component premises.

I could rewrite the sentence as:

If A and B then C

Propositional logic only cares about truth and how propositions fit together.

It doesn't matter what A, B or C stand for nor what they may mean -- from the perspective of propositional logic all that matters is there are two conjugated premises (A and B) and a conclusion (C) expressed in a conditional (if ... then ...). If one or both of the premises is false, then the conclusion must also be false. Why? Because the logical rules pertaining to conditionals and conjunctions make it that way! The brain twist is divorcing yourself from meaning -- just concern yourself with the structure of the argument and following the rules.

In exactly the same way the rules of Snap explain what must happen given a certain state of affairs, so the rules of propositional logic do the same with logical arguments. Another way to look at it is to remember that the rules of Snap don't care what the specific values of cards are, just that such values may match. Similarly, propositional logic doesn't care what the specific meanings of the premises or conclusion may be, just that there are such propositions connected in a structure that can be evaluated using rules that deal with just two possible states: true and false.

The simplest way to express the rules that govern logical operations for connecting propositions is with a truth table. Here's how "and" (conjugation) is defined, can you see how it works?

A B A and B
The definition of "and" (conjunction) as a truth table.
False False False
False True False
True False False
True True True

The first two columns represent propositions labelled A and B. The third column represents the outcome of the "and" operation. Each possible combination of the truth values of A and B is enumerated as a row, and the value for each row in the third "A and B" column defines what the outcome must be. It's a tabular way to express the rules of propositional logic. If you had any doubt how "and" worked, you'd find the definitive answer in this truth table.

For example, take the first row: if propositions A and B are both false (the first two columns), if they're evaluated as part of an "and" conjunction (the third column) the rule is that the result must be false.

Here's the truth table that defines the rule for another logical operation called "or" (disjunction):

A B A or B
The definition of "or" (disjunction) as a truth table.
False False False
False True True
True False True
True True True

Let's pretend that A is false but B is true. How would you evaluate truth value of "A or B"? Is it true or false?

The answer is that "A or B" is true. Why? Because the second line tells us so: the A column is false, the B column is true so the result, expressed in the third column, is true.

Here's one final truth table for you to ponder. It's a bit different to the other two since it only works on one premise.

A not A
The definition of "not" (negation) as a truth table.
False True
True False

It's easy to see what the "not" operation does to a proposition: it flips its truth value so false becomes true and true becomes false.

While these logical operations have familiar names ("and", "or" and "not") that appear to relate to how they work, it is the truth table and only the truth table that defines how they behave not any similarity to how we may use such words in everyday English.

Logical puzzles become fun when you combine logical operations to build more complicated structures. Take for example:

(A and B) or (C or not D)

I've put parenthesis around propositions so you can see how they relate to the logical operators. If we pretend all the propositions represented by letters are false, what is the overall truth value?

Before you answer, here's a hint to help work it out: there are four logical operations in the sentence -- an "and", "not" and two "or"s. The "and" operates on A and B whereas the "not" operates on D. The two "or"s are interesting: one of them operates on C and the result of not D (notice how the "not" needs to be evaluated before the enclosing "or" can be worked out). The other "or" operates on the result of the "and" operation and the previously mentioned "or".

Given the state of affairs where all the proposition letters represent false, how do we use the rules of propositional logic to evaluate the overall truth of the sentence?

We play the logical "game" in the same way we would with Snap: we just follow the rules. Here's how:

Start by evaluating the operators within the parenthesis. If we replace the propositions A and B with their truth values (remember, in this case, all the propositions are false), we get:

(false and false) or (C or not D)

Given the truth table described earlier, the first "and" operation evaluates to false because both the propositions are false. Here's how the sentence looks as a result:

false or (C or not D)

As I mentioned previously, to evaluate the "or" in the remaining parenthesis we should first evaluate the "not" operator. If D is false then the truth table for "not" tells us that "not D" must evaluate to true. Since C is false, the sentence looks like this when C and not D are replaced by their truth values:

false or (false or true)

Let's evaluate the "or" in the parenthesis. The truth table for "or" tells me that if one of the propositions is true then the "or" operation must evaluate to true, resulting in:

false or true

Re-using this rule again to evaluate the final "or" operator gives the result:

true

The only way to solve such logical puzzles is by following the inevitable steps dictated by the rules. If Logic provides an inevitable, almost mechanical framework for thinking about things (and propositional logic is only one type of logic we may use for this purpose) then we should be able to build machines to work with such seemingly mechanical logical rules.

It turns out that we can and that we call such machines computers!

You may be wondering why physical objects like computers can use such (apparently mental) models of logic and movements of thought. This brings us to our next brain twist.

Representation

We often pretend one thing represents something else.

For example, in the UK we know where the Queen is because the building, car, boat or aircraft containing the Queen always flies the royal standard. Put another way, the presence of the royal standard represents the presence of the Queen. This isn't a scientific fact -- it's not like the Queen is also a mysterious force of nature who manipulates flags and flag poles. Rather, we collectively understand the royal standard represents the Queen's presence and somebody, somewhere has the job to make sure flags are correctly aligned with what she's up to.

I imagine this sounds like a lot of trouble to take over a flag, especially when you consider the complicated and extensive travel plans of the Queen.

Instead, imagine Her Majesty insisted on a radical modernisation. Why not use electronics to show the Queen's presence at the touch of a button? Perhaps there could be a light to represent her status: if it were on, she was present.

Such a device could be built using the simple circuit diagram shown below:

The diagram is easy to read. At the top is the symbol representing a battery that supplies the electrical power for the components. Notice that it has a "+" sign to designate its positive end. Lines form a rectangle to join the battery to the other two components at the bottom. The circle containing an "X" represents a bulb and the gap created by the line veering off at an angle represents a switch.

The switch can be in two states: on (creating a circuit so electrical current moves through the components, thus lighting the bulb) or off (where the circuit is broken, stopping the electrical current and switching off the bulb).

Here's a royal brain twist for you: can you think of something else that deals with only two possible states?

If you thought, "propositional logic!" then you deserve a knighthood.

Why not use the on and off states of the circuit (as controlled by the switch) to represent true and false, respectively? When the tourist walking by Buckingham Palace wonders about the truth of the premise, "the Queen is home" they can check for the answer by looking at the bulb.

Here's another royal brain twist: it's possible to make simple circuits that mimic logical operations ("and", "or", "not" and so on).

The Queen has a husband -- the Duke of Edinburgh. Given the wonders of modern technology it's possible to light the bulb if both the Queen and her husband are present or if only one of them is present. The bulb could show:

  • The Queen is present and the Duke of Edinburgh is present;
  • The Queen is present or the Duke of Edinburgh is present.

Since you were paying attention to propositional logic you'll know that the two sentences are the same as: "A and B" and "A or B".
